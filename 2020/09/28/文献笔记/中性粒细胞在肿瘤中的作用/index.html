CXCR1 and CXCR2 Chemokine Receptor Agonists Produced by Tumors Induce Neutrophil Extracellular Traps that Interfere with Immune Cytotoxicity

Immunity, 2020

Neutrophils are the only cell type able to undergo a peculiar form of cell death called NETosis, as a
result of which the nuclear DNA of the neutrophil is extruded and projected into the tissular surroundings, where it can exert bactericidal properties. The majority of reports attribute pro-tumorigenic roles to neutrophils, including angiogenesis promotion and immunosuppressive
effects. Such extracellular DNA conjugated to neutrophil proteins is termed neutrophil extracellular traps(NETs), since it can entrap microorganisms. Tumors may cunningly pervert NETosis in their own interest. The tumor microenvironment is in fact very rich in factors that can induce NET extrusion from infiltrating neutrophils or GR-MDSCs. In this paper, the study documents the ability of tumors producing ELR+ CXCL chemokines to induce NETs in a manner dependent on CXCR1 and/or CXCR2. CXCR1 is definitely involved in the process, as verified with a specific neutralizing mAb. No specific blocking antibody against CXCR2 was available. Complete blockade with a CXCR1 agent does not exclude a role for CXCR2, since the biology of this receptor is complex and is known to form heterodimers with CXCR1. This study also showed that NETs might coat and shield tumor cells against cytotoxicity, as mediated by CD8+T cells and NK lymphocytes. Such a protective mechanism that results in armored tumor cells was lost when extracellular DNA was digested and involved less frequent cytotoxic contact between effector and target cells. In their experiments, pharmacological NETosis inhibition synergizes with the most potent immunotherapy strategy in the clinic combining anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 mAbs. The mechanism is envisioned to involve NET inhibition of the ensuing
cytotoxic effector T cell response following checkpoint inhibition.
